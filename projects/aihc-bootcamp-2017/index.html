Artificial Intelligence and Healthcare Bootcamp

Anand Avati, Pranav Rajpurkar, Nigam Shah and Andrew Ng

Description

Over the course of Fall Quarter 2017, our bootcamp covers a large breadth of topics at the intersection of artificial intelligence and healthcare. Students take a dive into cutting-edge research with medical imaging and Electronic Health Record data, working closely with Ph.D. students and each other.

    Exercise: Concept Distillation Within each reading, find 3 concepts to explain in your own words (12 total). The idea of this exercise is to be able to distill and communicate a machine learning technique into its core idea. We encourage you to pick concepts which you find particularly tough to understand, and want to explore more in depth. You'll present 2 concepts to the rest of the group for one minute each.

    Due: Thursday 10/5 at 1p

    Example: "Bayesian statistics describes an approach to integrate uncertainty into our understanding of the world. Let's say we want to understand how a system works. As Bayesians, we consider all possible designs of the system and decide how probable each possible design of the system is based on our beliefs ("prior"). We also compute the probability of our observations ("evidence") for each possible design ("likelihood"). We can then combine our prior knowledge and evidence to update our belief ("posterior") about the probabiltity of each design."

    Assignment: Patient Risk Prediction You're tasked with predicting the 10-year probability of heart attack for the general population. You've looked at literature and found that logistic regression is the most widely used method for making predictions. You're trying to test your hypothesis that neural networks can improve upon the performance of a logistic regression approach.

    Due: Saturday 10/7 at 11:59p

    Simulate a population of 10000 people with varying risks (probabilities) of heart attack. Each patient has 10 attributes that are continuous in the domain (0, 1), with 5 attributes responsible for risk, and 5 attributes that do not affect risk. First, design a risk function that has clinical backing. Then train a logistic regression classifier and NN classifier on the outcomes (not the risks!) of a subset of 1000 people. How do the classifiers compare? What attributes of the risk function affect the differences in performance between the classifiers?
